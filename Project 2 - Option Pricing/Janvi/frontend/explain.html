What Each Variable Means

S - Current Stock Price

The current market price of the underlying stock. Higher stock prices increase call option values and decrease put option values.

K - Strike Price

The price at which you can buy (call) or sell (put) the stock. This is fixed when you enter the option contract.

T - Time to Expiration

Measured in years. More time = more value because there's more opportunity for the stock to move in your favor.

r - Risk-Free Rate

The theoretical return on a risk-free investment (like Treasury bills). Usually around 3-5%. Affects the present value of the strike price.

œÉ (sigma) - Volatility

How much the stock price fluctuates, measured as annual standard deviation. Higher volatility = higher option prices (both calls and puts).

N(x) - Normal Distribution

The cumulative normal distribution function. This gives us probabilities based on the assumption that stock returns follow a normal distribution.

Intrinsic Value vs. Time Value

Intrinsic Value

The value if you exercised the option right now:

  • Call: max(S - K, 0)
  • Put: max(K - S, 0)

If the intrinsic value is zero, the option is "out of the money."

Time Value

The premium beyond intrinsic value that accounts for:

  • Time until expiration
  • Volatility
  • Interest rates
  • Potential for future profit

Time value = Total Option Price - Intrinsic Value

üí° Key Insight

At expiration, time value drops to zero! Your option is worth only its intrinsic value. This is why options lose value over time (theta decay).

Why Does Volatility Increase Option Prices?

Think of volatility as "potential." Higher volatility means the stock could move dramatically in either direction. Since options have limited downside (you can't lose more than the premium) but unlimited upside (for calls) or substantial upside (for puts), more movement = more profit potential.

Low Volatility (10%)

Stock likely stays near current price. Small chance of big moves. Lower option prices.

High Volatility (50%)

Stock could move significantly. Higher chance of profit. Higher option prices.

How Time Decay Works (Theta)

Every day that passes, your option loses some time value. This is called theta decay.

Time Decay Accelerates

Theta decay is not linear. Options lose value slowly at first, then rapidly in the final weeks before expiration. This is why many traders avoid holding options into the last 30 days.

Example: An option might lose $0.05/day with 60 days left, but $0.30/day with 10 days left.
